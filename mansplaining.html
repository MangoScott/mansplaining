It’s not the explaining.
It’s the assuming.

Mansplaining is what happens when an explanation is delivered with total confidence and an unchecked assumption about who in the room knows more. The term took off after Rebecca Solnit’s 2008 essay Men Explain Things to Me — in which a man lectured her about an important book she should really read. The book was hers.2 He hadn’t read it. He kept going. If you’ve ever watched Johannes explain Alexandra’s own job back to her — congratulations, you’ve seen it live.

Every signature feature.
None of them requested.

“Well, actually—” The two words that start it all.

Industry-leading interruption speed. Most recently deployed by Elijah to correct a statement of Alexandra’s that was already correct.

“Let me break it down for you.”

Delivered, with remarkable consistency, to Emily — who holds an advanced degree in the thing being broken down.

“It’s pretty simple, really.”

A reliable signal that Johannes has a simple understanding of a complicated subject.

“What she’s trying to say is—”

Seamless restatement. Alexandra’s point, re-presented thirty seconds later. Louder, and now Elijah’s.

“Have you tried turning it off and on?”

Asked of Emily, who wrote the documentation currently being paraphrased back to her.

Support.
For when it happens to you.

No single response fits every room — a panel, a dinner, a code review carry different stakes. Alexandra and Emily, this section is yours. Elijah and Johannes — take notes. Four field-tested options:

The credential drop

Calmly state your expertise and keep going. Alexandra’s signature move. Devastating in its simplicity.

“I’m familiar — I wrote it.”

The boomerang

Ask one question deeper than his explanation. The pause that follows is the lesson.

“And how does that handle the edge case?”

The name-and-move

Name the pattern without litigating it, then reclaim the floor. Best with witnesses.

“I’d like to finish my point.”

The ally assist

For bystanders: redirect credit to the person who actually said it first. Elijah, this one’s for you.

“That’s Emily’s point — Emily, keep going?”
